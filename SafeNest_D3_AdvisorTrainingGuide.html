The Foundation

Why the D3 report exists — and what it is not

Most real estate marketing gives buyers information that sells. The D3 Deep Research Report gives buyers information that decides. These are fundamentally different things.

The typical developer brochure shows an infinity pool, quotes a price per sft, and lists schools nearby. It never tells you that the developer is building their first project, that the JV has three landowners with undisclosed revenue-share terms, or that the construction is at Foundation Stage with 36 months of execution risk ahead. SafeNest exists because buyers in Hyderabad's luxury segment deserve the same analytical rigour applied to a stock investment — before committing ₹1–2 Cr.

What the D3 report is
A structured, evidence-based due diligence document for a single project. It draws from RERA filings, HMDA approvals, Encumbrance Certificates, legal opinions, developer brochures, and sanctioned plans. Every score is calculated by formula — no advisor judgment, no developer influence. The result is a buyer-safe narrative that helps a buyer decide, not a sales document that helps a developer sell.
What the D3 report is not
It is not a guarantee of project quality or delivery. It is not a legal document. It is not investment advice. It is not a credit rating. And it is absolutely not a tool to push a buyer toward booking — if the data shows a buyer's profile does not match a project, the D3 should help them decide not to book, and redirect them to a better fit.
Your Role

What you do — and don't do — with the D3

Your job is to be the translator. The D3 contains detailed analysis. Your buyer is a busy IT professional who has 90 minutes with you. The gap between the document and their decision is your value.

✓ Your Job
  • Explain what each score means in plain language
  • Connect the analysis to the buyer's specific situation
  • Surface the watch items clearly without alarm
  • Profile-match: "For your risk profile, this aligns because…"
  • Tell buyers when a project is NOT a good fit for them
  • Answer questions you know; defer what you don't
  • Guide buyers to the next steps checklist
✗ Never Do This
  • Show buyers Group 3 individual risk sub-scores
  • Show buyers JV Intelligence (Group 5) details
  • Use alarm language about risks
  • Rank one project as "better than" another
  • Share scoring rationale or negotiation leverage
  • Promise delivery or appreciation outcomes
  • Say "this is the best project in Kollur"
The Golden Rule
Every verdict must be framed as profile-matching, not quality-ranking. Say: "For a buyer who can wait 36 months and is comfortable with a first-project developer, this project aligns well." Never say: "This is better than Prestige or Aparna."

The SafeNest Score™ — What it is and how it works

The SafeNest Score™ is a weighted composite of 8 quality parameters, scored 0–10. It is the most important number in the report. A buyer will ask about it first.

The Formula
SafeNest Score™ Calculation
SafeNest Score =
(Legal_Title_Score × 0.18) +
(Developer_Track_Score × 0.15) +
(Construction_Progress_Score × 0.18) +
(Location_Quality_Score × 0.15) +
(Product_Quality_Score × 0.12) +
(Escrow_Financial_Score × 0.10) +
(JV_Structure_Score × 0.10) +
(Demand_Liquidity_Score × 0.02)

Notice the highest weights: Legal (18%) and Construction (18%). This is intentional. Legal problems can cost a buyer everything. Construction delays are the most common pain point in Indian real estate. Developer track record (15%) and Location (15%) follow. Demand and Liquidity (2%) is the lowest — because micro-market demand is the hardest to measure without verified booking data, and inflating it would introduce bias.

Inside the Score

The 8 parameters — what each one measures and why it matters

1. Legal & Title Score (weight: 18%) — The most important parameter
What it measures: Whether the land under the project is free from litigation, ownership disputes, and legal encumbrances. Sources: Encumbrance Certificate (EC), legal opinions, RERA title declaration, Prohibitory Orders register, mortgage status.

Why 18%? A project with a perfect product and a first-class developer becomes worthless if the land title is clouded. Legal problems are the most difficult and expensive to resolve post-booking. This parameter sets the floor — everything else rests on a clean title.

Scoring signals: 8–10 = RERA Active + clean EC + multiple independent legal opinions + no litigation. 5–7 = minor encumbrances present but resolvable. Below 5 = active litigation, disputed title, or missing legal documentation.
VIAN MRIDA example: 8/10 — RERA Active, 42-year EC across 27 entries, two advocate opinions, HMDA mortgages only (regulatory, not risk).
2. Developer Track Record (weight: 15%) — The trust anchor
What it measures: The developer's history of completing and delivering RERA-registered projects on time, at specification. Sources: RERA database of completed projects, MCA filings, builder reputation in market, bank approval history.

Why 15%? Land title and escrow protect your money. Developer track record determines whether the product you see in the brochure is the product you receive. A first-project developer has no delivery data — no basis for scoring beyond their structural setup.

Scoring signals: 8–10 = 3+ completed RERA projects delivered on time with buyer testimonials. 5–7 = 1–2 completions, some delays. Below 5 = first project or unverifiable track record.

Hard rule: The Developer Track Score is hard-capped at 5 for any developer building their first verifiable project, regardless of other credentials.
VIAN MRIDA example: 4/10 — First project explicitly declared in RERA. No completed deliveries to examine. MCA not independently confirmed at time of scoring.
3. Construction Progress Score (weight: 18%) — The time risk
What it measures: How far along the physical construction is — from Foundation Stage to Occupancy Certificate. This is measured entirely by physical evidence, never by the RERA portal's percentage figure.

Why 18%? Construction Stage is the single biggest predictor of when you will receive your home — and whether you will receive it at all. A project at Finishing Stage has almost no delivery risk. A project at Foundation Stage carries the full weight of 36+ months of execution uncertainty.

The 5 stages and scores:
Foundation Stage: 2/10 (deterministic — no discretion)
Structure WIP <50%: 4/10
Structure WIP >50%: 6/10
Finishing: 8/10
OC Received: 10/10

Critical rule: Never use the RERA portal's construction percentage to determine the stage. Only physical evidence — site visits, dated photographs, slab completion evidence — determines the stage.
VIAN MRIDA example: 2/10 — Foundation Stage. No physical construction evidence in any document. This is the correct score per protocol.
4. Location Quality Score (weight: 15%) — The address
What it measures: ORR access distance, proximity to IT employment hubs (in km and commute time), schools within 2 km, hospitals within 5 km, and the micro-market's general position in Hyderabad's growth trajectory.

Why 15%? Location is the one thing the developer cannot change after construction. A poorly located project with a great product is still a poor investment. Location quality directly affects rental demand, resale liquidity, and daily livability.

Key benchmarks: ORR ramp within 1 km = strong; 2–3 km = acceptable; beyond 3 km = watch. IT hub within 10 km direct = ideal; 15–20 km via ORR = acceptable if commute time is under 25 min. Hospital within 5 km = benchmark for this price band.
VIAN MRIDA example: 7/10 — ORR ramp 1.25 km, Financial District 13 km / 20 min, DPS ~1 km. Docked 3 points: nearest hospital is ~15 min (~7.5 km), outside the 5 km benchmark for this price band.
5. Product Quality Score (weight: 12%) — What you live in
What it measures: Construction method (Mivan vs RCC), ceiling height, unit size range, amenity specification (clubhouse, pool, gymnasium), corridor widths, fitting brands, and overall specification relative to the price band and micro-market.

Why 12%? Product quality determines daily lived experience and long-term resale value. A well-specified product in a weaker location can still be a compelling buy. Product is what the Luxury Index captures in even more detail.

Construction method scoring: Mivan = 10 base. Precast/Steel Frame = 6 base. RCC (conventional) = 4 base.
VIAN MRIDA example: 7/10 — Mivan confirmed, 20 ft lobby, 40,000 sft sky clubhouse, 112.4 sft/unit amenity. Docked 3 points: parking is 1:1 (minimum standard) and ceiling height ~9.7 ft is unconfirmed by developer.
6. Escrow & Financial Score (weight: 10%) — The money safety
What it measures: Whether a proper RERA-compliant escrow is in place, whether the bank accounts are named and verifiable, what percentage is ring-fenced for construction, and how many scheduled banks have approved home loans.

Why 10%? The escrow is your financial protection. A single dedicated account is the minimum. Three accounts at a scheduled bank with named account numbers in the RERA filing is exceptional — it means 70% of your payment is legally unreachable by the developer for any non-construction purpose.

Scoring: 3-account escrow + named bank + home loans from 2+ banks = 9–10. Single account present = 6–7. No escrow confirmation = below 5.
VIAN MRIDA example: 9/10 — 3-account Axis Bank escrow with all account numbers in the RERA filing. 70% ring-fenced. HDFC, Axis, Kotak home loans approved. This is the strongest score in the project.
7. JV Structure Score (weight: 10%) — The land deal complexity
What it measures: The Joint Venture arrangement between the developer and landowner(s). Is it a clean outright purchase? A registered DAGPA (Development Agreement cum General Power of Attorney)? How many landowners? Are revenue share terms disclosed?

Why 10%? Land disputes between developers and landowners are one of the most common causes of construction delays and legal complications in Hyderabad. A single landowner with a registered DAGPA is lower risk than three private individuals with undisclosed revenue-share terms.

JV Classification Rules (Rule A through D): Registered DAGPA = baseline 7. Cap at 7 regardless of other factors. Three or more private individual landowners = deduct 1 from baseline. Revenue share terms undisclosed = further watchpoint.
VIAN MRIDA example: 4/10 — Registered DAGPA (Rule A, cap 7). Three private individual land owners (Rule D, -1 from baseline). Revenue share not disclosed. Score: 4.
8. Demand & Liquidity Score (weight: 2%) — The market signal
What it measures: How actively the micro-market is transacting, how quickly similar properties are being absorbed, and whether the project itself has verified booking velocity (with evidence).

Why only 2%? Demand data is the easiest to manipulate — developers frequently claim "80% sold in weekend launch" with no verification. SafeNest keeps this weight low precisely to prevent inflated demand signals from masking poor fundamentals.

Hard cap rule: This score is hard-capped at 5/10 for any project without independently verified booking data. No exceptions. Market fundamentals alone can earn up to 5. Verified booking velocity earns 6–10.
VIAN MRIDA example: 5/10 — Hard cap applied. Kollur micro-market fundamentals are solid (ORR corridor, 12% CAGR) but no project-specific verified booking data exists.

How to explain the verdict to a buyer
Highly Favorable (8+): "The project checks almost every box — strong legal foundation, experienced developer, and good construction progress. For your profile, this is a low-risk commitment."

Favorable (6.5–7.9): "There are genuine strengths here — location, product, and legal standing are all solid. There are one or two areas to watch, and I'll walk you through them. For a buyer comfortable with those watchpoints, this aligns well."

The SafeNest Luxury Index™ — Scoring what you live in

The Luxury Index scores the physical product — the apartment itself, the building, the amenities, and the land use. It uses 5 weighted pillars, each scored out of 20 points, for a maximum of 100.

Think of the SafeNest Score as measuring whether a project is safe to invest in. The Luxury Index measures what quality of living that project delivers. These are different questions. A project can have a Conditional SafeNest Score (risk concerns) but a high Luxury Index (excellent specification). This is common at Foundation Stage — the risk is timing, not product quality.

5 Pillars of the Luxury Index

What each pillar measures and how it is scored

Pillar 1: Construction (max 20 pts)
Three sub-scores combine to give this pillar its 20 points:

Construction Type: Mivan = 10/10. Precast/Steel Frame = 6/10. RCC (conventional) = 4/10.
Why it matters: Mivan produces monolithic concrete walls that eliminate the seepage risk common in brick-and-mortar construction. For a buyer planning 10+ years of ownership, this is structural quality that compounds.

Ceiling Height: ≥11 ft = 10/10. ≥10.5 ft = 8. ≥10 ft = 6. ≥9.5 ft = 4. Below 9.5 = 2.
Why it matters: Ceiling height is one of the most tangible luxury markers. You feel it the moment you walk in, and it cannot be retrofitted later.

Stilt Height: ≥24 ft = 10/10. ≥20 ft = 8. ≥16 ft = 6. ≥12 ft = 4. Below = 2.
Why it matters: Stilt height signals the lobby and entry experience. A 20+ ft stilt is a premium arrival experience; a 10 ft stilt is just parking.
Pillar 2: Density (max 20 pts)
Three sub-scores: Units per acre, Units per floor, Lifts-to-units ratio

Density (units/acre): <60 = 10/10. <100 = 8. <150 = 6. <180 = 4. 180+ = 2.
Why it matters: This is the single most important quality-of-life metric that marketing materials hide. Lower density = more open space, less traffic, quieter, better resale.

Units per floor: ≤2 = 10/10. ≤6 = 8. ≤10 = 6. ≤12 = 4. 12+ = 2.
Why it matters: How many neighbours does the lift open onto at your floor? 4 units/floor vs 13 units/floor is a different living experience entirely.

Lift ratio (units per lift): <1.5 per lift = 10/10. Down to <4 per lift = 2.
Why it matters: In a 32-floor tower, lift wait time during peak hours is a real daily friction. Projects that under-spec lifts create community complaints within 12 months of possession.
Pillar 3: Amenities (max 20 pts)
Three sub-scores: Amenity sft per unit, Open space ratio, Premium Feature 1 presence

Amenity sft per unit: ≥150 = 10/10. ≥100 = 8. ≥60 = 6. ≥40 = 4. Below = 2.
Why it matters: Belt average in Kollur is ~22 sft/unit. 100+ sft/unit means a meaningful clubhouse that residents actually use.

Open space ratio (% of land area): ≥75% = 10/10. ≥60% = 8. ≥50% = 6. ≥40% = 4.
Why it matters: How much of the land is buildings vs open — gardens, walkways, play areas. A 78% open space ratio is exceptional.

Premium Feature 1 presence: If Premium_Feature_1 is populated = 10. Blank = 5.
Why it matters: Premium Feature 1 is the project's standout differentiator — an infinity pool at 280 ft, a rooftop helipad, a private cinema. Its presence signals a developer investing in signature specification.
Pillar 4: Spatial (max 20 pts)
Three sub-scores: Minimum unit size, Corridor width, Door height

Unit size (min sft): ≥2,500 = 10/10. ≥1,800 = 8. ≥1,600 = 6. ≥1,200 = 4. Below = 2.
Why it matters: Minimum unit size sets the price band and lifestyle floor for the entire project. A project where the smallest unit is 1,395 sft is targeting a genuinely different buyer from a project starting at 750 sft.

Corridor width: ≥10 ft = 10/10. ≥8 ft = 8. ≥6 ft = 4.
Why it matters: Corridor width is visible every day. 10 ft corridors feel like hotel hallways — premium. 4–5 ft corridors feel cramped. Note: corridor width is not entered in Premium Features — it has its own dedicated field.

Door height: ≥9 ft = 10/10. ≥8 ft = 8. ≥7 ft = 6. Below = 2.
Why it matters: Standard doors are 7 ft. 8 ft doors are rare and visually striking. 9 ft doors in a luxury apartment are a genuine rarity.
Pillar 5: Infrastructure (max 20 pts)
Three sub-scores: Parking ratio, Floor count, Land per unit (sft)

Parking ratio (covered/unit): ≥2.0 = 10/10. ≥1.5 = 8. ≥1.2 = 6. ≥1.0 = 4. Below = 2.
Why it matters: 1:1 is the minimum. A 2-car household in a 1:1 project pays extra for a second slot or parks on the road — a daily friction that compounds.

Floor count: ≥35 floors = 10/10. ≥20 = 8. ≥12 = 6. Below = 4.
Why it matters: Vertical mass signals investment in foundation, structure, and vertical infrastructure. 32 floors in Kollur is the tallest project in the micro-market — a structural statement.

Land per unit (sft): ≥1,000 = 10/10. ≥600 = 8. ≥400 = 6. ≥300 = 4. Below = 2.
Why it matters: How much land does each resident effectively "own" a share of? Higher land per unit means more space per resident — gardens, breathing room, and resale stability.

How to explain the Luxury Index to a buyer
"The Luxury Index scores what you actually live in — the building, the apartment, and the amenities — on 100 points across five dimensions. VIAN MRIDA scores 68 out of 100, which puts it in the Premium segment. That is the value proposition of the ORR corridor at this price: you get Luxury-quality product specs at Premium-density pricing."

The Risk Score & Risk Verdict — Advisor use only

Critical: Field Visibility
The 7 individual risk sub-scores and the Internal_Risk_Score (the calculated number) are never shown to buyers. The buyer sees only the Risk Verdict in verbal form: "Low Risk", "Moderate Risk", "High Risk", or "Critical Risk". This rule is non-negotiable.

The risk score uses an inverted scale: 10 = safest, 0 = most dangerous. This is the opposite of the quality scores. A risk sub-score of 8 means low risk in that dimension. A risk sub-score of 2 means high risk.

Internal Risk Score Formula
Internal_Risk_Score = ROUND(
  (Risk_Litigation + Risk_RERA_Compliance + Risk_Developer_Tier +
   Risk_JV_Structure + Risk_Construction_Stage +
   Risk_Escrow_Diversion + Risk_Delay_History) ÷ 7, 2)

Note that the Risk Verdict bands use different thresholds from the Quality Verdict — this is intentional. Risk and quality are separate axes. A project can be Favorable quality with Moderate Risk (strong product, first-project developer), or Conditional quality with Low Risk (weaker location, but proven developer and strong construction progress). Always present both verdicts and explain what each measures.

How to explain Moderate Risk to a buyer
"The Risk Verdict of Moderate Risk reflects two specific facts about this project right now — the developer is building their first project, and construction is at Foundation Stage. Both of these will naturally improve as time passes. As floors rise and the developer proves their execution, the risk profile strengthens. The escrow protection score is excellent — your money is structurally protected. The risk here is not about losing money; it's about the 36-month execution journey with an unproven team."
Report Section 1

The Life Vision Block — Why it leads

The first section of every D3 is a 4-sentence vision paragraph. It is the only narrative writing in the report — deliberately positioned before facts, scores, or analysis.

The 4-sentence structure and why each matters
Sentence 1 — Commute: How does this home change the daily journey to work? Anchors the location benefit in daily lived reality.

Sentence 2 — Home: What does coming home feel like? Connects the physical product to emotion.

Sentence 3 — Community: What kind of people live here? Validates social fit.

Sentence 4 — Daily Life: One specific, vivid daily ritual. The detail that makes the abstract feel real.

When presenting the report, read or paraphrase the Vision Block first — before scores, before prices. It sets the emotional frame. Then move to the analysis. This order is important: emotion first, then logic validates the emotion.

Report Section 2

Basic Facts & Specs — What to walk through

This section is the factual anchor. Key items to emphasise in a client meeting:

1
RERA Active status — Always confirm this first. It is non-negotiable. RERA Active means the project is legally registered, the escrow is operational, and the developer is legally accountable for delivery.
2
Construction stage — Be direct. "Foundation Stage" means the buyer will not see floors rising for 6–9 months. This is the honest context for a 36-month possession timeline.
3
Units per floor — Flag this proactively. 13 units/floor is higher than typical luxury projects (4–8 units/floor). A buyer who values privacy should know this before booking.
4
Amenity ratio — This is VIAN MRIDA's standout strength. 112.4 sft per unit vs ~22 sft belt average is a 5× multiple. Make it concrete: "That is the difference between a shared treadmill room and a 4-floor sky clubhouse."
5
Mivan construction — Many buyers have heard the term but don't know what it means. Explain: "Mivan uses aluminium formwork to pour the entire floor — walls, slab, everything — in one continuous concrete pour. No bricks, no mortar joints. This eliminates the seepage risk that comes back to haunt buyers 5–7 years after possession in conventional construction."
Report Section 3

Price — Always all-in, no exceptions

Every price in the D3 report is the all-in total: base price + amenity charges + car parking + GST 5% + registration 7.6%. When presenting price, always state this explicitly.

✓ Say This
  • "The all-in price for a 2BHK is approximately ₹1.13 Cr. That includes everything — GST, registration, parking. No surprises."
  • "Your 20% at agreement is ₹22.6 lakhs on a ₹1.13 Cr unit. The balance of 70% is drawn progressively via your home loan."
  • "Maintenance after possession is estimated at ₹4–6 per sft per month — separate from the purchase price."
✗ Never Say
  • "The price is ₹6,600 per sft" — this is the base rate only, not what the buyer pays
  • "You can negotiate the price" — you never recommend negotiation tactics
  • "The price will go up 20% once Structure is complete" — do not promise appreciation
The CLP payment flow — know this by heart
Token: ₹50,000 to block the unit
Agreement (20%): Due at Agreement of Sale signing — this is where the escrow kicks in
Construction Linked (70%): Drawn via home loan against construction milestones — the developer cannot draw this money unless construction has progressed
Possession (10%): Due at Occupancy Certificate + key handover
Registration: GST 5% + Registration 7.6% at the registration stage

This is the safest payment structure for a buyer. The 70% is never released in bulk — it drips against verified construction progress.
Report Section 4

Quality Assessment — The heart of the D3

This section contains the SafeNest Score breakdown, the Luxury Index with pillar scores, the radar chart, and the construction progress indicator. It is the longest and most detailed section. Allocate 10–15 minutes here.

The radar chart — what it shows
The radar chart plots 6 of the 8 quality parameters against a regular hexagonal grid. A large, symmetrical hexagon = balanced, low-risk profile. A collapsed or irregular shape = concentration of risk in specific dimensions.

For VIAN MRIDA, the chart shows a pronounced collapse toward the Construction and Developer axes — with strong vertices at Legal, Escrow, and Location. This is the classic early-stage, first-project shape: financial protections are solid, but execution credibility is yet to be established. The shape itself tells the story more powerfully than any sentence.

Report Section 5

Watch Items — How to present concerns without alarming

Watch Items are the 3–4 specific things that triggered a score threshold or represent a verified gap in the documentation. They are not "red flags" — they are verification tasks with a specific action attached.

Watch Item trigger thresholds (score-based, not judgment)
Watch Items are auto-generated when specific score thresholds are crossed — there is no advisor discretion:

Developer Score < 6 → Developer Watch Item triggered
Construction Score ≤ 4 → Construction Stage Watch Item triggered
JV Score < 7 → JV Structure Watch Item triggered
Escrow Score < 7 → Escrow Watch Item triggered
Legal Score < 7 → Legal Watch Item triggered
Possession > 30 months → Long Possession Watch Item triggered

Each Watch Item must include the triggering evidence and a specific buyer action.
How to introduce Watch Items
"This section is not meant to worry you — it is meant to make you a more prepared buyer. Every Watch Item has a specific action you can take to verify it. Let me walk you through them one by one."
✓ Framing That Works
  • "These are things we found that need your attention before you sign."
  • "The developer is first-project — which means you should verify their MCA filing. Here is exactly how to do that."
  • "This is a verification task, not a deal-breaker. Most buyers complete this in 15 minutes."
✗ Framing to Avoid
  • "There are some serious concerns you should know about."
  • "This is risky because of the first-project developer."
  • "I would be careful about this JV structure."
Report Section 7

Suitability Table — The self-check tool

The suitability table is a buyer self-assessment. Seven binary questions. The more "Yes" answers, the stronger the profile-fit. Use this as a conversation prompt, not a score.

How to use the suitability table in conversation
Ask the buyer to go through the questions aloud with you. When they say "No" to a question, that is a conversation starter:

"You said you cannot wait 36 months — so possession timeline is a priority for you. That puts this project outside your ideal window. Let me show you two projects at Structure stage that would fit your timeline better."

The suitability table is the most powerful tool for redirecting a buyer to a better-fit project without making them feel they were shown the wrong thing. It makes the rejection data-driven, not opinion-driven.
Report Section 8

FAQ — Pre-answers to common objections

Every D3 includes 5 fixed questions and one conditional question (Q6 — triggers if construction is at Foundation or early Structure stage). Know the answers by heart — buyers who are seriously considering booking will ask these.

QuestionThe Core Answer
Q1 — Can I trust a first-project developer?Focus on structural protections: HMDA permit, 3-account escrow, bank approvals. Verify MCA filing — that is the buyer's job.
Q2 — What if possession is delayed?TSRERA delay penalty = SBI MCLR + 2% per month on all paid amounts. Full refund option also available. Penalty clause must be in Agreement of Sale.
Q3 — Is ₹6,600/sft a fair price?Fair for Foundation Stage + this specification in Kollur. Comparable Mivan projects with proven developers are priced ₹1,000–1,500/sft higher.
Q4 — What is maintenance after possession?Estimate ₹4–6 per sft per month for a project of this specification. ~₹5,600–8,400/month for 1,395 sft.

Report Feature

The Calculators — Two financial intelligence tools

Every D3 embeds two interactive calculators pre-filled with the project's data. These are advisor-facing tools — you explain the output, the buyer sees the numbers.

Calculator 1: True Cost Calculator
Shows the all-in financial picture over the full purchase and construction period: all-in price, EMI during construction, interest serviced before possession, and net outflow by possession day.

When to use: When a buyer says "the price seems reasonable" — the True Cost Calculator shows what they are actually committing to financially, including the interest cost during the 36-month construction period.

Pre-filled with: All-in price, handover timeline, CLP payment structure, rental estimate for opportunity cost. Buyer adds their loan percentage and interest rate.
Calculator 2: OTP vs CLP Calculator
Compares one-time payment (OTP) with construction-linked payment (CLP). Shows the breakeven point — the OTP discount level at which paying upfront becomes financially better than keeping money invested and paying via CLP.

When to use: If a developer introduces an OTP discount option. Many buyers assume a 5% OTP discount is always worth it — the calculator often shows the opposite, because keeping money in FD or equity during a 36-month construction period generates more than the discount saves.

Note: VIAN MRIDA currently offers CLP only. This calculator is relevant if/when an OTP option is introduced.
Client Conversations

Handling hard questions — word-for-word guidance

"Why should I trust SafeNest? You earn commission from developers."
"That is the most important question you can ask, and the answer is structural, not just a claim. SafeNest earns the same commission rate regardless of which project you select — so we have no financial reason to push you toward one project over another. Our commercial interest is in you making a decision you are confident in, because that is what builds our reputation. The D3 report you are holding is generated by formula, not by judgment — and the scores cannot be adjusted for any project, including this one."
"The report is Conditional. Does that mean I shouldn't buy?"
"Conditional means specific conditions need to be met before you commit — not that the project is unsuitable. Think of it as a checklist, not a verdict. If you complete the Verdict Conditions — the MCA check, the site visit, the lawyer review of the Agreement of Sale — and they all come back clean, the Conditional score has been addressed. A Conditional project with verified due diligence is often a better buy than a Favorable project you bought without looking closely."
"Is this project better than [competitor project]?"
"That is not the question I can answer — because 'better' depends entirely on your situation. What I can tell you is what this project offers and how it matches your specific profile. You told me you need possession within 36 months and you are comfortable with a first-project developer — let me show you the suitability table for this project and the one you're comparing, and we can look at where each fits your priorities."
"Can I negotiate the price?"
"The D3 report gives you the factual context for that conversation — including the developer's stage, the market CAGR, and what comparable projects are priced at. The negotiation itself is between you and the developer. I am not the right person to advise on negotiation strategy — what I can do is make sure you go into that conversation with the most accurate picture of the project's value."
"The developer told me they have already sold 60% of units. Why isn't that in the report?"
"We apply a hard cap on Demand score without independently verified booking data — which means we cannot reflect a developer's sales claim in the scoring. If the developer can show us RERA quarterly reports with verified unit inventory, we can revisit that score. Without verified data, including unverified booking claims would make every project look more attractive than the data supports."

Why these rules exist
Raw scores without context create fear. A buyer who sees a 4/10 Developer Score without the explanation framework will panic. The D3 report translates the data into narrative context — your job is to deliver that narrative, not the spreadsheet behind it. Sharing raw sub-scores bypasses the interpretation layer that makes the data useful rather than alarming.

How to present the D3 report — the meeting flow

1
Set the context (2 min): "I have completed SafeNest's full due diligence on this project. What you're about to see is our D3 Deep Research Report — it covers everything from the land title to the construction method to a full financial analysis. The scores are generated by formula, not by opinion."
2
Read or paraphrase the Vision Block (2 min): Let the imagination anchor before the analysis begins. Never lead with scores.
3
Show the cover scores and explain (3 min): SafeNest Score = quality assessment. Risk Verdict = risk assessment. Luxury Index = product quality. These are three different questions with three different answers.
4
Walk through Basic Facts and Specs (5 min): Focus on RERA status, construction stage, units/floor, amenity ratio, construction method.
5
Explain the price section (5 min): Always all-in. Walk through the CLP payment flow. Ask the buyer to confirm their loan pre-approval status.
6
Quality Assessment — the deep dive (10–15 min): Score by score. Use the radar chart. Construction progress bar. Keep the language simple and concrete.
7
Watch Items (5–8 min): Frame as verification tasks with actions. Never alarm. Always provide the specific action.
8
Suitability Table (2–3 min): Go through together. Listen for "No" answers — they are your pivot opportunities.
9
Next Steps (2 min): Review the checklist. Confirm which actions the buyer will take and by when. Offer to schedule a follow-up after the MCA check and site visit.
Summary Rules

The non-negotiable rules

Things that are never acceptable, regardless of situation
1. Showing risk sub-scores to buyers — The individual 7 risk parameter scores are advisor-only, always.
2. Showing JV Intelligence to buyers — Landowner names, revenue share terms, DAGPA details are advisor-only, always.
3. Using alarm language — "This is risky", "I would be worried about", "This could be a problem" — never.
4. Ranking projects — "This is better than X" or "X is worse than this" — never.
5. Making promises — Price appreciation, rental yield, possession on time — never promise any outcome.
6. Fabricating data — If you don't know, defer. If a data point is missing, say it is missing. Never fill gaps with assumptions.
